Account summary

Solenis makes the chemicals that paper mills use to turn wood pulp into paper, tissue, cardboard and packaging. That is its main business and where it earns most of its money. Over the last few years it has bought several other companies and grown fast, paying for those deals partly with borrowed money. So today it is a large, confident company that is also careful with cash. Its core paper market is changing, and its main new bet, special coatings that let paper replace plastic, works in the lab but is not yet selling at scale. Helping it work out who will actually buy, and how quickly, is where we come in.

The company is careful with money, and here is why

Solenis is owned by Platinum Equity, an investment firm that buys companies, improves them and later sells them on. To buy Solenis and the businesses added since, it borrowed heavily, and that debt has to be paid down. That is why S&P gives it a B- score, and why in September 2025 one of its companies raised about $2.55bn of new debt to help pay for NCH. None of this means trouble. Solenis is profitable and has been named a US Best Managed Company by Deloitte and the Wall Street Journal for six years running. But it does not have easy growth pulling it along, and in Europe high energy costs have squeezed its profit to somewhere around 8% to 12% by our reading.

What this means for us. Solenis watches every pound it spends. It will not pay for broad research or open-ended advice. It pays for small, fast work that protects its profit, helps it make one clear decision, or lowers the risk on money it has already spent. So our first offer should be small, quick, and end in a plain yes or no.

What sets the timing

From 12 August 2026, new EU packaging rules start, and on the same day a strict limit on PFAS chemicals in food packaging takes effect, with no grace period afterwards. PFAS are the long-lasting chemicals that many current coatings rely on, so this rule pushes brands towards paper and towards PFAS-free coatings. Solenis already knows the rule inside out. The useful question is not the rule itself, but which parts of it actually change what customers buy, and where.

How we write the emails

Each email should show that we understand the person's job and the decisions they are working on, and that we have real depth in their area. It should not simply repeat facts about their company that they already know.

People notice straight away when an email was not really written for them. So in every email the useful part is our reading of their situation, the part they may not have put into words yet, tied to the specific job they do. We make two or three connected points rather than one, so the email shows range, and we say plainly what we do in their area so the credibility is clear without selling.

The rules we follow

1

Do not repeat their own company facts

They already know their plants, products and the rules that affect them. Repeating those back is the clearest sign an email was not written for them.

2

Make two or three connected points

Each point should speak to part of what this person is responsible for, so the email shows real depth in their area rather than a single observation.

3

Only mention a regulation if it carries a consequence they may have missed

The useful part is what the rule means for their job, not the rule itself.

4

Connect the points to something real about them

Tie the observations to a detail from their own background or role, so it is clear the email was written for them and no one else.

5

Show the relevant experience plainly

Establish credibility with the specific work we do in their area. State it as something we do, not as a boast, so the depth is clear without selling.

6

Do not promise anything

Do not say we will prove or show them something. Say what we understand and how we might help, and stop there.

7

Keep it readable

Three or four short paragraphs. Every line should be clear on the first read, with no dramatic phrasing.

8

End with a clear ask for a meeting

Close by asking for a short meeting at a time that suits them, in a way that makes it easy to say yes. Avoid soft phrases like comparing notes.

9

Decide who not to email

If a person is hard to reach with a relevant message, or cannot influence the kind of work we sell, leave them.

A note on the subject line

Write the subject line last, once the email is settled. It should say the main point in plain words, so the person can see why it is worth opening. A subject that only hints at a topic, without the point, gives no reason to read on.
