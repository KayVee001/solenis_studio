Where external pressure is visible

Two public signals show the company is exposed to costs outside its control. Solenis applied a minimum 5% temporary surcharge in the United States from May 2025 because of tariff-driven costs, and a global energy surcharge of 8% to 14% from April 2026 in response to Middle East logistics and energy disruption. At the same time the paper and packaging market in Europe is uneven rather than booming, with weak packaging demand reported at DS Smith in late 2024 and continued pressure in European papermaking through 2025.

What this means for us. We should not pitch a general paper-market recovery story, because the market is not recovering evenly. Any proposal has to be tied to the specific applications where customers are still releasing investment despite the wider strain. That discipline is what makes a proposal credible to someone living the cost pressure.

Sources. Solenis surcharge announcements, April 2025 and March 2026. Reuters on DS Smith, December 2024. Financial Times on Italian papermaking, March 2025.

The rule that sets the timing

The clearest near-term trigger is the European packaging regulation, the PPWR. It entered into force on 11 February 2025 and generally applies from 12 August 2026, and it restricts PFAS in food-contact packaging once set thresholds are exceeded. This is what pushes brands and converters towards paper and towards PFAS-free coatings, which is the heart of the Solenis packaging offer. Solenis already knows the rule. The useful question is which parts of it actually move customer buying, and for which formats.

Source. European Commission packaging-waste page. The exact PFAS threshold figures should be legally checked against the regulation text before being quoted numerically in a client document.

The first paid project to propose

A six-week study, Europe first, that prioritises paper-based food-contact packaging applications for barrier coatings. One buyer, the VP or GM for Consumer Solutions across EMEA and Asia. Three application clusters at most, for example cupstock, grease-resistant foodservice papers, and one coated paperboard format chosen with the buyer. It would weigh regulatory urgency, converter and printer readiness, customer qualification needs, the competitor and partner picture, switching economics, and the right order to act in. It ends with a clear recommendation on where to win first. It deliberately excludes whole-company strategy, hygiene, mining, data centres, global roll-up, technical reformulation and plant modelling. It is kept to one decision and one buyer, because that is what a company with no prior relationship to us can actually fund.

What not to say

Four things would fail the evidence and credibility tests. Do not speak as if Solenis is a publicly reported company with open business-unit financials. Do not claim a formal segment structure that the company does not disclose. Do not imply that the packaging regulation automatically guarantees large near-term revenue. And do not pitch a whole-company strategy study as the first engagement. Each of these would mark us out as not understanding either the company or the rules.

What still needs checking before use

Being straight about what we do not yet know is part of the credibility. The brief flags the following as not reliably public, to be confirmed before any of it is used in a client document.

Money and structure. Current debt, leverage and any credit rating were not reliably available from high-confidence public sources, and must not be inferred from older drafts or web summaries. Segment profitability and regional revenue mix are not disclosed.
The exact rule detail. The exact PFAS threshold figures in the packaging regulation should be legally checked against the regulation text before being quoted numerically.
The named buyer. The public record shows a Consumer Solutions regional leadership role, but not a full org chart for who owns barrier-coatings decisions. The specific person and title should be confirmed.
How the buyer frames the issue. Whether they see it as regulatory readiness, commercial prioritisation, partnership choice, or innovation sequencing. The project holds across all four, but the opening language should be tuned once this is known.

How we write the emails

Every email is written to one senior leader as if by another. It earns a reply by noticing something useful about their area that they may not yet have put into words, not by repeating facts they already know. The shape below is a tried and tested way to open a conversation with a senior decision maker, and it is close to how the leading strategy firms do it.

The order matters. We lead with a signal, not a summary. We frame an opportunity worth exploring, not a verdict on their business. We show relevant experience plainly, ask for a short meeting with confidence, and close warmly. Every email stays close to 180 words, so it reads in under a minute.

How the account intelligence becomes an email

Every email is the end of a short pipeline that starts with the account brief and ends at one decision a specific person owns. The steps below are the same for the four roles on the Contacts tab and for any contact added later, so the substance is always traceable rather than improvised.

1

Start from one verified brief

The account brief is the only source of core intelligence. Nothing enters an email that is not in the brief, or confirmed from a company or regulator source. Where the brief is silent, the email stays silent, which is what keeps it accurate.

2

Map each role to the one decision it owns

We do not write to a person in general. We find the single decision in the brief that sits closest to their remit, for example which paper formats to prioritise, or how hard to invest in data-centre cooling. That decision becomes the centre of the email, which is why each contact is tied to a wedge.

3

Draw the pain points from the brief, not from guesswork

For that decision, we take the specific tensions the brief describes, the things that make the decision hard. These become the pain points shown on each contact. Each one traces back to a line in the brief, so it reads as real to the person rather than a generic problem any supplier could name.

4

Match the pain points to a small set of recurring themes

The brief surfaces a handful of themes that run across the whole account: the packaging rule and its timing, a visible barrier-coatings offer, the move into data-centre cooling, growth built by acquisition, and visible cost pressure. We map each role's pain points to the one or two themes that speak to them, and carry only those into the email so it stays focused.

5

Turn the theme into a signal they may not have framed yet

The substance of the email is one uncommon read of that theme, an insight about their area rather than a fact about their company. For the packaging role, it is that the move away from PFAS is several formats moving at different speeds, not one switch. That line is the part that earns the reply, and it is the only place the email tries to add something new.

6

Write to the decision, then confirm the person

The email is written to the decision and the role. The exact person and current title are confirmed through People Data Labs before sending, so the message is right even before the name is filled in.

The rules we follow

1

Open with a signal, not a summary

One uncommon, relevant observation about their area of focus. Never a compliment, never a recap of their own company.

2

Do not repeat their own facts

Their acquisitions, plans, products and the rules that affect them are background, used to find a tension worth raising. They are never read back to them.

3

Never diagnose or lecture

No lines like your hardest problem is, or the real issue is. We raise areas worth exploring, not conclusions about their business.

4

Stay measured

Confident but not absolute. Phrasing such as one area that may be worth comparing notes on keeps it honest and avoids overclaiming.

5

Establish credibility in one line

We work with their peers and leading players in adjacent spaces, which gives a useful outside view. Specific, and never name-dropping without approval.

6

Ask for 30 minutes, with confidence

To share what we are seeing and compare perspectives, stating plainly that we are sure it will be worth their time. Never apologetic, and never the line not a sales call.

7

Keep it short and plain

Around 180 words, a few short paragraphs, every line clear on the first read. No marketing language and none of the usual AI words.

8

Choose who not to email

If a person cannot influence the kind of work we sell, we leave them rather than spend credibility on a message that will not land.

A note on the subject line

Write the subject line last, once the email is settled. Keep it simple, specific and relevant, the way a colleague would write it. Avoid anything that reads like an article headline or a campaign line, which gives no reason to trust what is inside.
